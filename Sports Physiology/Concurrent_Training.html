Navigating the Interference Effect in Concurrent Training

Elite slalom and cyclical athletes face a critical programming challenge: developing massive aerobic/anaerobic engine capacity while simultaneously maximizing neurological power output. This dashboard synthesizes current sports science literature to provide actionable guidelines for pairing gym modalities with high-intensity water sessions.

📊

The Core Problem

High metabolic stress (lactate, VO2 blocks) actively inhibits the body's ability to synthesize new muscle tissue and build explosive power if programmed incorrectly.

🏋️

The Optimal Gym Focus

During intense metabolic water blocks, gym sessions must prioritize low-volume, high-load Maximal Strength or high-velocity Power Development to rely purely on neural drive.

⚠️

The Danger Zone

Pairing high-lactate water sessions with high-repetition Power Endurance in the gym creates overlapping peripheral fatigue, ensuring severe overtraining and blunted adaptations.

Scheduling Optimization: The Recovery Timeline

When conflicting stimuli are unavoidable, the intra-session rest period is the primary modifier of the interference effect. The chart below models the restoration of strength/power adaptation potential over time following an endurance session.

🔄 Rule 1: Sequence

If same-day training is required, perform Gym (Strength/Power) first. Neural fatigue from water sessions severely blunts force production.

⏱️ Rule 2: The 6-Hour Rule

AMPK activity remains elevated for several hours. A minimum of 6 hours of passive recovery and fueling is required to restore mTOR sensitivity.

🎯 Rule 3: Microdosing

During intense lactate phases, limit gym work to 20-30 minute "microdoses" of pure neural stimulation (e.g., 3x3 max strength) to maintain tissue quality.
