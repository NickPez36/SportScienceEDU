⚡ ATP-PCr (Phosphagen) System

Section Purpose: Here we break down the most immediate energy source available to the elite athlete. This section separates the system into its two trainable components: Alactic Power (the peak rate of ATP resynthesis) and Alactic Capacity (the total pool of available phosphocreatine). Understanding this distinction dictates whether you prescribe ultra-short explosive bursts or slightly longer sustained sprints.

Maximal Rate of Energy Release

Alactic power refers to the absolute maximum rate at which the phosphagen system can split ATP and utilize Phosphocreatine (PCr) to generate force. It dictates an athlete's peak explosiveness in the first 1-3 seconds of movement.

  • ✔ Goal: Maximize neural drive, motor unit recruitment, and enzyme kinetics (Creatine Kinase).
  • ✔ Work Duration: 1 to 5 seconds.
  • ✔ Rest Ratio: 1:15 to 1:20+ (Near complete recovery to prevent glycolytic shift).
  • ✔ Intensity: 100% (Maximal Intent).

Practical Implementation

Training must be ceased if output/speed drops by even 3-5%, as this indicates neural fatigue and a shift towards secondary energy systems.

Example Protocol 3 x 4x 15m Max Sprints Rest: 2 min b/w reps, 8 min b/w sets

Total Phosphagen Pool

Alactic capacity focuses on increasing the total muscular stores of ATP and PCr, allowing the athlete to sustain high-power outputs for slightly longer (up to 10-12 seconds) before glycolysis fully dominates.

  • ✔ Goal: Increase intramuscular substrate stores and delay the drop-off in power curve.
  • ✔ Work Duration: 7 to 12 seconds.
  • ✔ Rest Ratio: 1:10 to 1:12.
  • ✔ Intensity: 95-100%.

Practical Implementation

Slightly longer efforts push the system to complete depletion. Nutrition (Creatine Monohydrate supplementation) heavily influences this specific trait.

Example Protocol 5 x 8-10 sec Resisted Sprints Rest: 2.5 - 3 minutes b/w reps

🩸 Glycolytic (Lactic) System

Section Purpose: Often misunderstood as a single "lactic" bucket, this section delineates the glycolytic system into Production (the speed of glycolysis) and Tolerance (managing the resulting acidosis). Coaches will learn to manipulate work-to-rest ratios to either increase the engine's RPMs (production) or increase the chassis's ability to handle the toxic byproducts (tolerance).

Maximizing Glycolytic Flux

Training the rate at which glucose is broken down. The goal is to produce massive amounts of energy (and consequentially, lactate and H+ ions) in a short time. This requires high intensity and full clearance between reps to ensure the enzymes (like PFK) aren't inhibited by prior acidosis.

Physiological Target:

Upregulation of glycolytic enzymes (Phosphofructokinase). High CNS output.

  • Duration: 15 - 30 seconds
  • Intensity: 95 - 100%
  • Rest: 1:5 to 1:8 (e.g., 30s work, 3-4 min rest) - allows buffering before next rep.

Buffering & Clearance

Training the body to maintain mechanical output amidst high localized acidosis. This is achieved via incomplete rest periods, forcing the muscle to work while pH is dropping, which upregulates buffering agents and MCT (Monocarboxylate) transporters.

Physiological Target:

Intracellular buffering (carnosine, bicarbonate), MCT1/MCT4 transporter density, mental fortitude.

  • Duration: 30 - 90 seconds
  • Intensity: 85 - 95%
  • Rest: 1:1 to 1:3 (e.g., 60s work, 60-120s rest) - incomplete recovery is mandatory.

🫀 Oxidative (Aerobic) System

Section Purpose: A robust aerobic system is the foundation of high-performance recovery and sustained output. This interactive breakdown separates cardiovascular delivery (Central adaptations like heart structure) from muscular extraction (Peripheral adaptations like mitochondria and capillaries). Targeting the right component relies heavily on intensity manipulation.

🫀

Central Adaptations (Delivery)

"Building a bigger pump and wider pipes."

Focuses on the cardiovascular system's ability to deliver oxygenated blood to working tissues. Driven by long-duration, low-to-moderate intensity work that maximizes venous return and cardiac stretch.

Key Mechanisms

  • Left Ventricular Eccentric Hypertrophy (chamber stretching).
  • Increased blood plasma volume.
  • Increased total stroke volume (SV).

Training Theme

Cardiac Output Method (LSD): 60-90+ minutes at HR 130-150 bpm (Zone 2). Continuous, cyclical work.

🧬

Peripheral Adaptations (Extraction)

"Building more efficient cellular factories."

Focuses on the local skeletal muscle's ability to extract and utilize the delivered oxygen. Driven by higher intensity intervals near or slightly above the anaerobic threshold.

Key Mechanisms

  • Mitochondrial biogenesis (increased size and density).
  • Capillary network proliferation (angiogenesis).
  • Increased oxidative enzymes (Succinate dehydrogenase).

Training Theme

Aerobic Power/VO2max Intervals: e.g., 4 x 4 minutes at >90% HRmax with 2-3 min active rest.

🚀 Neuromuscular Speed Domains

Section Purpose: Shifting from metabolic energy systems to neuromuscular capabilities, this section maps the kinematics of a sprint. Elite performance requires specific training for each distinct phase: overcoming inertia (Acceleration), achieving peak velocity (Max V), and minimizing deceleration (Speed Tolerance). The graph visually plots this velocity curve over distance.

Velocity over Distance: The Sprint Curve

1. Acceleration

0 to ~20/30 meters

Physics: Overcoming inertia. Requires high concentric force production and longer ground contact times to push horizontally.

Training Themes
  • • Heavy sled pushes/pulls
  • • Hill sprints (short, steep)
  • • Broad jumps / Med ball throws

2. Max Velocity

30 to ~60 meters

Physics: Maintaining momentum. Dominated by vertical force, minimal ground contact time, and high CNS elasticity (SSC).

Training Themes
  • • Flying 10s/20s (Fly-ins)
  • • Over-speed / Assisted towing
  • • Intensive plyometrics (Bounds)

3. Speed Tolerance

60+ meters

Physics: Delaying deceleration. The ability to maintain optimal mechanics and velocity despite CNS and metabolic fatigue.

Training Themes
  • • Intensive tempo runs (80-90%)
  • • Repeated Sprint Ability (RSA)
  • • 150m - 300m interval repeats
